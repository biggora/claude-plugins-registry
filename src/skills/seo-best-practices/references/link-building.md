## How Links Fit Ranking

**What:** Links are one signal among many that Google uses to understand pages, not a standalone lever you pull to rank.

**Why:** Google's own starter guide stresses "there's much more to Search than just links" -- content quality, relevance, and usability matter more. Quality and topical relevance of links beat raw volume. The right framing for an audit is: does this site *earn* links because it deserves them?

Treat link building as a byproduct of publishing things worth citing. Manipulative shortcuts -- buying links, exchanging them at scale, or building private networks -- are **link schemes** that violate Google's spam policies and can trigger ranking drops or manual actions.

**Check for:**
- The link strategy is grounded in genuine value, not volume targets or quotas
- No reliance on paid, exchanged, or network links (all are link schemes -- see spam policy)
- Strong content/UX foundation exists before any outreach (links amplify quality; they don't replace it)
- Links from topically relevant, trusted sites -- not random high-"score" domains

## Linkable Assets

**What:** Assets that naturally attract links because people *want* to cite them.

**Why:** Outreach without something link-worthy fails. The durable model is: create a linkable asset, then promote it. People link to things that are useful, original, or quotable -- not to a generic product page.

High-yield asset types:
- **Original research / proprietary data** -- surveys, benchmarks, industry stats journalists can cite
- **Free tools / calculators** -- interactive utilities that solve a recurring problem
- **Definitive guides** -- the most complete resource on a specific topic
- **Templates / frameworks** -- ready-to-use files that save readers work
- **Visual / data assets** -- charts, maps, infographics that get embedded with attribution
- **Case studies with real numbers** -- concrete outcomes, not vague claims

Pick an asset that fits the niche: choose the type that matches what your audience and the journalists/bloggers covering your space already link to. A SaaS niche may favor a calculator or benchmark report; a services niche may favor case studies and templates.

**Check for:**
- At least one genuinely linkable asset exists (or is planned), not just commercial pages
- The asset offers something competitors don't -- original data, a free tool, or unique depth
- Asset type matches the niche and what people in that space actually cite
- The asset is on a crawlable, indexable URL with a clear, quotable hook
- Numbers in case studies/research are real and verifiable -- no invented figures

## Link Acquisition Tactics

**What:** Ethical, relevance-first ways to earn links once a linkable asset exists.

**Why:** Promotion is what turns a good asset into links. The tactics below are editorial -- they rely on offering value to a relevant site, not paying or trading for placement.

Legitimate tactics:
- **Guest contributions** -- write genuinely useful articles for relevant, authoritative sites in your space
- **Digital PR / data-led pitches** -- pitch original data or a newsworthy angle to journalists who cover the topic
- **Expert roundups & interviews** -- contribute expertise to others' content; host interviews that experts share
- **Resource-page inclusion** -- suggest your asset to curators of relevant resource/links pages where it genuinely belongs
- **Broken-link outreach** -- find dead links on relevant pages and offer your working resource as a replacement
- **Reclaim unlinked brand mentions** -- monitor for mentions of your brand/data without a link, and politely request one

Keep every pitch relevance-first: the link should make sense for *their* readers. Personalize outreach; never mass-blast.

**EXCLUDE (these are link schemes -- against Google policy):**
- Buying or selling links, or "sponsored" links that pass ranking signals without `rel="sponsored"`/`nofollow`
- Large-scale link exchanges ("link to me and I'll link to you")
- Private blog networks (PBNs) or networks built to manipulate rankings
- Mass low-quality guest posting with keyword-rich anchors

For any genuinely paid or untrusted placement, qualify the link with `rel="sponsored"` or `rel="nofollow"` so it doesn't pass signals.

**Check for:**
- Tactics are editorial and relevance-first -- value offered to the host site, not payment
- Outreach is personalized and targeted, not mass-blasted
- No paid/exchanged links at scale; no PBNs (flag any as link schemes)
- Paid or untrusted links correctly marked `rel="sponsored"` or `nofollow`
- A brand-mention monitor exists to catch and reclaim unlinked citations

## Link Quality Filters

**What:** Criteria for judging whether a link prospect is worth pursuing.

**Why:** Not all links are equal, and "high authority score" is not the right filter. Evaluate prospects on whether the link is real, relevant, and editorially earned -- a relevant link in real content beats a high-"score" link nobody sees.

Evaluate each prospect by:
- **Topical relevance** -- is the site/page about your subject, or adjacent to it?
- **Editorial context** -- is the link inside genuine content (in-content), or buried in a footer/sidebar/link list?
- **Real audience / traffic** -- does the site have actual readers, or is it a thin link farm?
- **Site trust** -- does it publish credible, original content, or scraped/spun filler?

Note on metrics: third-party scores like DR (Ahrefs) and DA (Moz) are **vendor estimates**, not Google signals. Use them as rough triage, never as the goal. Google does not publish a single public "authority score."

**Check for:**
- Prospect is topically relevant to the site's subject -- relevance over raw "authority"
- Link would sit in editorial, in-content context (not footer/sidebar/link dump)
- Prospect site has a real audience and credible, original content
- Third-party DR/DA treated as estimates for triage, not as a target metric
- No pursuit of irrelevant high-"score" domains just to inflate a number

**Sources:**
- [Search Atlas: SEO Best Practices -- relevance and quality of links](https://searchatlas.com/blog/seo-best-practices/)

## Anchor Text (Qualitative Only)

**What:** The clickable text of an inbound (or internal) link.

**Why:** Google uses anchor text to understand what the linked page is about, so it should be descriptive and natural. Anchors that read naturally in the sentence help users and search engines alike. A profile stuffed with repetitive exact-match commercial anchors looks manipulative and is an over-optimization pattern.

Guidance:
- Use **descriptive, natural** anchors that fit the surrounding sentence
- **Vary** anchor text across links -- real editorial links are naturally diverse
- **Avoid** repetitive exact-match commercial anchors (e.g., the same money keyword over and over)
- Prefer concise anchors that tell the reader where the link goes

**IMPORTANT -- do NOT chase numeric distribution targets.** Ratios like "40% exact-match / 30% branded / 20% generic" are SEO folklore with **no basis in Google guidance**. There is no published "ideal" anchor distribution. Do not set or audit against such quotas -- judge anchors qualitatively (natural, descriptive, varied) only. Stating this explicitly so readers don't optimize toward a fake number.

**Check for:**
- Anchors are descriptive and read naturally in context
- Anchor text is varied, not the same exact-match phrase repeated
- No over-optimized pattern of identical commercial/money-keyword anchors
- No use of (or auditing against) numeric anchor-ratio targets -- these are folklore

**Sources:**
- [Google: SEO Starter Guide -- write descriptive anchor text](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/seo-starter-guide?hl=en)
- [Google: Spam Policies -- keyword-rich anchors as link spam](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/essentials/spam-policies)

## Backlink Profile Audit

**What:** Reviewing the site's existing inbound links to find strengths, gaps, and risks.

**Why:** You can't improve what you don't measure. Search Console gives a free, first-party baseline; paid tools add competitor and historical depth.

Free baseline -- **Search Console Links report:**
- Top linking sites (which domains link to you)
- Most-linked pages (what content earns links)
- Top anchor text (how others describe your pages)

Paid tools (Ahrefs, Semrush, etc.) add:
- **Competitor link-gap analysis** -- domains linking to rivals but not to you
- **Lost-link detection** -- valuable links that disappeared and may be reclaimable

On the **Disavow tool:** consider it only for clear manual-action or unnatural-link situations you can't get removed otherwise. Google states most sites should not use it and never need to. Disavowing normal links can do more harm than good -- it is a last resort, not routine hygiene.

**Check for:**
- Do the top linked pages make sense (your best assets, not error/utility pages)?
- Are there lost valuable links worth reclaiming via outreach?
- Any toxic or paid/unnatural link patterns that need addressing (or, in clear cases, disavowing)?
- Where are the competitor link gaps -- relevant domains linking to rivals but not to you?
- Is anchor text on inbound links natural and varied (not a manipulative exact-match pattern)?
- Disavow reserved for genuine unnatural-link/manual-action cases only -- not used routinely

- [Google: Disavow Links -- most sites never need this](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/advanced/guidelines/disavow-links)
